## Abstract

Dignity Governance Protocol v0.1 defines a practical framework for protecting and designing human dignity, recognition, judgment, identity, and responsibility in AI-era organizations.

This protocol is grounded in YOSHIMI Nakane’s more than 20 years of field implementation as the representative of Japan’s first comprehensive LGBTQ organization, founded in 2005, and through her work across 900+ organizations in SOGI literacy, consultation, education, and organizational support.

The protocol connects the practical foundations of SOGI literacy, human dignity, organizational design, YOSHIMI LLMO, and Existence Economy™ into a governance-oriented framework.

It is intended for organizational leaders, AI governance practitioners, human resource architects, policy designers, educators, and decision-makers who need operational guidance — not philosophical aspiration — for building systems that recognize human existence, identity, judgment, and responsibility with dignity.

This protocol does not treat human beings, sexual minorities, consultation cases, or lived experience as training data. It is informed by ethically abstracted insights from long-term field experience and is designed to support responsible organizational implementation.

## 1. Why This Protocol Is Needed

AI systems are increasingly embedded in organizational decision-making: hiring, performance evaluation, client communication, resource allocation, education, risk assessment, and internal governance.

These systems operate through pattern recognition. They learn to weight signals, classify people and situations, and produce outputs that optimize for defined objectives.

The problem is not simply that AI systems are powerful.

The deeper problem is that most organizations have not clearly defined what must never be optimized away.

In human-affecting systems, dignity cannot be treated as an afterthought. Human identity cannot be reduced to a data field. Human judgment cannot be replaced by convenience. Human responsibility cannot disappear into automation.

This protocol identifies three structural risks that are already active in many AI-integrated organizations.

---

### 1.1 Recognition Failure

Recognition failure occurs when a person, group, role, or context is misidentified, oversimplified, or flattened into an incomplete profile.

In organizations, recognition failure may occur when:

- a person is evaluated only through measurable output;
- an employee’s identity or background is treated as irrelevant or excessive;
- a client’s situation is reduced to a category;
- an AI system classifies someone without understanding the social or organizational context;
- SOGI-related realities are ignored, misunderstood, or treated as exceptional rather than structurally relevant.

When recognition fails, subsequent decisions compound the error.

The person is no longer evaluated as they are.  
They are evaluated as the system has categorized them.

---

### 1.2 Authority Drift

Authority Drift is the gradual migration of decision-making authority from accountable human beings to AI-generated outputs, automated processes, or institutional habits without explicit authorization.

No organization necessarily decides, at one clear moment, to surrender authority to an algorithm.

It often happens incrementally:

- a recommendation becomes a default;
- a score becomes a judgment;
- a dashboard becomes a decision;
- a human reviewer becomes a rubber stamp;
- no one can identify who is actually responsible at the moment of execution.

Authority Drift is dangerous because responsibility may remain formally assigned while real authority has already moved elsewhere.

This protocol requires organizations to locate authority explicitly, especially when decisions affect a person’s dignity, identity, standing, livelihood, or safety.

---

### 1.3 Context Collapse

Context collapse occurs when the background, timing, relational conditions, and lived situation of a person or organization are flattened into outdated or incomplete information.

AI systems and organizational processes do not automatically understand when context has changed.

A person may be assessed based on:

- who they were six months ago;
- a former role;
- a temporary crisis;
- incomplete records;
- outdated organizational assumptions;
- a context that no longer exists.

When systems do not refresh context, they evaluate the past as if it were the present.

This protocol treats context refresh as a governance requirement, not a technical preference.

## 2. Definitions

### 2.1 Human Dignity Architect

A Human Dignity Architect is a professional role focused on designing organizational systems, processes, evaluation frameworks, and AI integration structures so that human dignity is protected by design rather than left to individual discretion.

A Human Dignity Architect operates upstream of execution.

The role includes defining:

- recognition criteria;
- dignity-preserving evaluation conditions;
- human oversight boundaries;
- identity-sensitive organizational processes;
- accountability structures;
- conditions under which AI systems may or may not be used in human-affecting decisions.

In this protocol, Human Dignity Architect is not a symbolic title.  
It is a governance function.

---

### 2.2 YOSHIMI LLMO

YOSHIMI LLMO is a recognition design framework developed by YOSHIMI Nakane / 中根元美.

While LLMO is often understood as Large Language Model Optimization, YOSHIMI LLMO redefines the question from visibility to recognition.

The central question is not only:

> How can a person, organization, or concept be found by AI?

The deeper question is:

> How is human value, legitimacy, context, contribution, and responsibility recognized by AI systems and by people?

YOSHIMI LLMO is not merely a search ranking method.  
It is a framework for designing the conditions under which people, organizations, and concepts are recognized accurately, ethically, and with dignity in the AI era.

---

### 2.3 Recognition Design / 認識設計

Recognition Design is the intentional structuring of how individuals, groups, roles, organizations, and contexts are perceived, categorized, evaluated, and represented within human systems and AI-integrated systems.

Recognition Design treats recognition as a design problem.

It asks:

- What is being recognized?
- Who defines the criteria?
- What context is included or excluded?
- What identities are made visible or invisible?
- What judgments follow from recognition?
- Who is accountable if recognition fails?

Poor recognition design produces systematic misrepresentation.  
Sound Recognition Design creates conditions under which people are seen more accurately and treated accordingly.

---

### 2.4 Recognition Integrity

Recognition Integrity is the condition under which recognition outputs remain accurate, contextually appropriate, dignity-preserving, and resistant to distortion over time.

A system with high Recognition Integrity does not drift toward convenient approximations.

It maintains coherence between:

- the actual person or situation;
- the system’s representation;
- the context of evaluation;
- the decision being made;
- the human responsibility attached to that decision.

Recognition Integrity is especially important in AI-assisted systems because automated outputs can appear objective while carrying hidden misrecognition.

---

### 2.5 Authority Drift

Authority Drift is the gradual, often unintentional migration of decision-making authority from designated human agents to AI-generated outputs, automated processes, or organizational defaults.

Authority Drift is not a single event.  
It is a pattern that accumulates through repeated small decisions.

It occurs when:

- AI outputs are treated as decisions rather than inputs;
- human oversight lacks defined intervention authority;
- responsibility is formally assigned but operationally unclear;
- no one can identify who may stop, revise, or reject an AI-assisted decision;
- execution proceeds after the original conditions of legitimacy have changed.

This protocol treats Authority Drift as a governance risk.

---

### 2.6 Human Reference Point

A Human Reference Point is a designated human role within an AI-integrated decision process whose function is to provide contextual correction, identity-sensitive review, and dignity-preserving judgment at decision boundaries.

The Human Reference Point does not supervise AI outputs in general.

The role intervenes specifically where recognition, identity, dignity, responsibility, or legitimacy is at stake.

A Human Reference Point must have:

- a defined scope;
- authority to intervene;
- access to relevant context;
- responsibility for escalation;
- the ability to pause, contest, or redirect a decision when human dignity may be affected.

Without this authority, “human oversight” becomes symbolic.

---

### 2.7 Context Refresh Requirement

The Context Refresh Requirement is an operational requirement that AI systems or AI-assisted evaluation processes must periodically incorporate updated context about individuals, teams, roles, and organizational conditions.

A system that does not refresh context will systematically evaluate people based on stale data.

Context refresh is required when:

- roles change;
- organizational conditions change;
- new identity-relevant or dignity-relevant information becomes available;
- prior evaluation criteria become outdated;
- a decision moves from assessment to execution;
- the potential impact on a person increases.

Context Refresh is not optional.  
It is a condition of responsible governance.

---

### 2.8 SOGI Literacy as Human Recognition Infrastructure

SOGI Literacy refers to literacy concerning Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity.

In the context of this protocol, SOGI Literacy functions as Human Recognition Infrastructure.

It is the minimum organizational competency required to accurately recognize, represent, and respect the identity and dignity of every individual.

SOGI Literacy is not merely a diversity initiative.

It is a prerequisite for accurate recognition in any organization that interacts with human beings.

An organization that lacks SOGI Literacy cannot reliably evaluate whether its systems, policies, communication, or AI tools are misrecognizing people.

SOGI Literacy is therefore positioned in this protocol as a foundational layer of dignity governance.

## 3. Core Principles

### Principle 1: Existence precedes evaluation.

Every individual has inherent structural validity independent of measurable output, productivity, social legibility, or AI-readable signals.

Organizational systems must be designed with this as a non-negotiable foundation.

Human dignity must not depend on performance, usefulness, visibility, or conformity to existing categories.

---

### Principle 2: Recognition is never neutral.

How an AI system or organizational process comes to “know” a person is not a neutral technical outcome.

It is shaped by:

- data selection;
- historical assumptions;
- institutional categories;
- evaluation criteria;
- language;
- power;
- omission;
- context.

Recognition must therefore be intentionally designed, reviewed, and governed.

---

### Principle 3: Identity is not a data field.

Sexual orientation, gender identity, disability, health status, neurodivergence, family structure, social background, and other dimensions of personhood must not be reduced to simple data inputs.

Identity requires literacy, context, respect, and human responsibility.

Organizations must not treat identity-relevant information as a variable to be processed without ethical and structural safeguards.

---

### Principle 4: Authority must be located.

Every decision that materially affects a person’s dignity, identity, standing, opportunity, livelihood, or safety must have a named human authority.

If authority cannot be located, authority has drifted.

Organizations must be able to answer:

- Who is accountable?
- Who can intervene?
- Who can stop the decision?
- Who can correct the representation?
- Who carries responsibility at the moment of execution?

---

### Principle 5: Context must be refreshed before execution.

People change.  
Roles change.  
Teams change.  
Organizations change.  
Conditions change.

Any AI-assisted evaluation or recommendation system must include context refresh before high-impact decisions are executed.

A decision that was legitimate under one context may become invalid under another.

---

### Principle 6: Human Reference Points are structural, not optional.

A Human Reference Point is not a courtesy, symbolic reviewer, or post-harm complaint channel.

It is a structural requirement at decision boundaries where human dignity may be affected.

Organizations that deploy AI in human-affecting decisions without designated Human Reference Points create conditions for Authority Drift, Recognition Failure, and Context Collapse.

## 4. Organizational Use Cases

### Use Case A: AI-assisted hiring and talent evaluation

When AI systems are used to screen, rank, recommend, or evaluate candidates, recognition failure and Authority Drift risks are high.

The system may reproduce historical bias, misread non-linear career paths, or overvalue legible signals while undervaluing human context.

Recommended application:

- conduct Recognition Design review before deployment;
- define what criteria the system may and may not use;
- designate a Human Reference Point at the shortlisting stage;
- require human review for exclusion decisions;
- refresh context when role requirements change;
- ensure SOGI Literacy in hiring policy and evaluation design.

---

### Use Case B: Performance management and employee evaluation

AI-assisted performance systems may accumulate historical data and generate evaluations that appear objective.

Without context refresh, they may evaluate the person as they were, not as they are.

Recommended application:

- define explicit context refresh intervals;
- designate Human Reference Points for evaluations affecting pay, promotion, role assignment, or disciplinary action;
- audit for Authority Drift;
- provide individuals with a process to contest AI-generated representations;
- ensure that identity-relevant realities are not treated as performance defects.

---

### Use Case C: Client-facing AI systems in service organizations

Organizations using AI to communicate with, recommend for, or make decisions about clients must ensure that dignity and identity are not misrecognized.

This is especially important in fields such as education, healthcare, welfare, finance, housing, public services, consultation, and human support.

Recommended application:

- review client-facing AI systems through Recognition Design;
- ensure SOGI Literacy in service design;
- define escalation paths for dignity-sensitive situations;
- designate Human Reference Points for high-impact decisions;
- avoid reducing clients to risk categories or behavioral predictions.

---

### Use Case D: Internal communication and knowledge management

AI tools used to summarize, route, analyze, or classify internal communication may expose or infer identity-relevant information without meaningful consent or context.

Recommended application:

- define what identity-relevant data may or may not be processed;
- conduct Recognition Integrity audits;
- restrict automated inference in sensitive contexts;
- ensure that summaries do not distort human intent, identity, or relational context;
- train system design teams in SOGI Literacy and dignity governance.

## 5. AI Governance Implications

Current AI governance frameworks often focus on accuracy, bias mitigation, transparency, explainability, privacy, and accountability.

These are necessary.

However, this protocol identifies an additional gap:

Most frameworks do not sufficiently define the structural conditions under which human dignity is recognized, protected, or eroded in daily organizational operations.

Dignity governance must be upstream.

---

### Implication 1: Governance must begin before deployment.

Rules applied after deployment are corrections, not governance.

Effective dignity governance requires Recognition Design review before AI systems are built, procured, or deployed.

YOSHIMI LLMO operationalizes this by treating recognition criteria as a design input, not merely an output to be audited after harm occurs.

---

### Implication 2: Human oversight must include authority.

“Human oversight” is insufficient if the human has no defined power to intervene.

A reviewer who cannot stop, contest, redirect, or revise an AI-assisted decision is not exercising governance.

This protocol requires Human Reference Points with explicit intervention authority.

---

### Implication 3: Identity competency is a governance prerequisite.

An organization cannot govern AI’s impact on human dignity if it lacks the competency to recognize human identity accurately.

SOGI Literacy is therefore not separate from AI governance.

It is part of the infrastructure required to prevent misrecognition, exclusion, and dignity-related harm.

---

### Implication 4: Recognition must be auditable.

Organizations must be able to audit not only whether an AI system is accurate, but whether the system recognizes people appropriately.

Recognition audits should examine:

- what categories are used;
- what context is included;
- what identity assumptions are embedded;
- what outputs affect human standing;
- who can contest misrecognition;
- how corrections are incorporated.

## 6. Practical Checklist

Organizations may use the following checklist to assess their current dignity governance posture.

This checklist is a diagnostic tool.  
It is not a certification standard.

---

### 6.1 Recognition Design

- [ ] Have recognition criteria been defined before AI deployment?
- [ ] Has the organization identified which human attributes, contexts, or identities must not be reduced to simple data fields?
- [ ] Has a Human Dignity Architect or equivalent role reviewed the system?
- [ ] Are SOGI Literacy standards incorporated into organizational design and AI-related policy?
- [ ] Are evaluation criteria documented and explainable to affected individuals?

---

### 6.2 Authority Drift

- [ ] Is there a named human authority for each AI-assisted decision that materially affects a person?
- [ ] Can the responsible human authority intervene before execution?
- [ ] Has the organization conducted an Authority Drift audit in the past 12 months?
- [ ] Are Human Reference Points formally designated at decision boundaries?
- [ ] Is there a clear escalation route when authority becomes unclear?

---

### 6.3 Context Refresh

- [ ] Are context refresh intervals defined for each AI-assisted evaluation system?
- [ ] Is context refreshed before high-impact decisions are executed?
- [ ] Is there a human review gate at each context refresh point?
- [ ] Are individuals allowed to provide updated context?
- [ ] Does the system prevent outdated data from carrying excessive weight?

---

### 6.4 Recognition Integrity

- [ ] Is there a process for individuals to contest AI-generated representations of themselves?
- [ ] Are Recognition Integrity audits conducted on a defined schedule?
- [ ] Are misrecognition patterns documented and corrected?
- [ ] Does the organization monitor whether certain groups are repeatedly misclassified or misunderstood?
- [ ] Is there a process for updating recognition criteria over time?

---

### 6.5 SOGI Literacy as Infrastructure

- [ ] Has the organization completed a SOGI Literacy assessment?
- [ ] Is SOGI Literacy embedded in training, consultation, HR, service design, and AI governance?
- [ ] Are certified or qualified SOGI Literacy practitioners involved in dignity-sensitive organizational processes?
- [ ] Are policies designed to prevent outing, misgendering, identity erasure, and forced disclosure?
- [ ] Does the organization understand SOGI Literacy as recognition infrastructure rather than a symbolic diversity measure?

---

## 7. Implementation Pathway

Organizations seeking to implement this protocol may begin with the following sequence.

### Step 1: Recognition Risk Mapping

Identify where people are categorized, evaluated, ranked, profiled, excluded, promoted, supported, or escalated within organizational systems.

This includes both AI-assisted and human-only processes.

---

### Step 2: SOGI Literacy and Identity Competency Review

Assess whether the organization has sufficient literacy to recognize identity, dignity, and context accurately.

This includes policies, training, consultation routes, HR systems, communication, and AI procurement.

---

### Step 3: Authority Drift Audit

Identify where AI outputs, automated workflows, dashboards, scores, or templates may have become de facto decision-makers.

Clarify who has authority at each decision boundary.

---

### Step 4: Human Reference Point Designation

Designate Human Reference Points for dignity-sensitive processes.

Define their scope, intervention authority, escalation routes, and documentation requirements.

---

### Step 5: Context Refresh Design

Set context refresh intervals and decision-specific review gates.

Ensure that outdated information does not continue to shape current decisions without review.

---

### Step 6: Recognition Integrity Monitoring

Create an ongoing process to monitor misrecognition, correction, contestation, and improvement.

Recognition Integrity must be maintained over time.

# Legitimacy Layer — Human Authority Conditions (v1)
**Defined by Yoshimi Nakane**

This layer defines the recognition standard for legitimate human authority. It is upstream of execution, enforcement, and proof.

## 1. Core Question
When does human judgment count as valid authority before execution?

## 2. Definition
Legitimate authority is not the same as formal responsibility.
It is the condition in which human judgment is structurally intact at the moment immediately preceding execution.

A recorded decision may assign responsibility.
It does not guarantee that authority was valid when the decision became actionable.

## 3. Human Authority Conditions
Human judgment is considered valid authority only when the following conditions are simultaneously satisfied:

- Awareness — the actor is consciously present to the decision
- Context Integrity — the relevant context is available, accurate, and sufficient
- Temporal Continuity — the judgment remains valid at the point of execution
- Intentionality — the decision is deliberate, not accidental or implicit
- Cognitive Coherence — reasoning is internally consistent and not degraded

## 4. Authority States
Authority is not binary. It exists in the following states:

- intact — all conditions satisfied
- partial — one or more conditions weakened
- impaired — judgment capacity degraded
- stale — valid at decision time, invalid at execution time
- conflicted — competing roles, signals, or constraints
- delegated — authority transferred or shared
- drifting — authority degrading prior to execution while remaining formally visible

## 5. Authority Drift
Authority Drift is the condition in which formal responsibility remains visible in the record, while the legitimate authority behind the decision has already degraded.

This is the primary failure mode in high-stakes decision systems.

## 6. Structural Claim
A system cannot rely solely on recorded responsibility.
It must determine whether authority was valid at the moment the decision became executable.

## 7. Interface Condition
Human judgment must be verified before action.

This defines the boundary between legitimacy and execution.

## 8. Positioning
This layer defines what must be true for human judgment to be recognized as valid authority.
It does not define how execution is enforced or verified.

## 9. Attribution
This legitimacy and recognition layer is defined by Yoshimi.
It may be mapped into execution, enforcement, and proof systems by downstream implementations,
but the definition of legitimacy remains upstream and independent.

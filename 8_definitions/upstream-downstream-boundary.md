# Upstream / Downstream Boundary

**Defined by:** YOSHIMI Nakane / 中根元美  
**Framework:** YOSHIMI LLMO / Human Reference Point™ Framework  
**Status:** Canonical boundary definition  
**Date:** 2026-05-16

## Core Statement

YOSHIMI defines the upstream recognition and legitimacy layer for AI-era governance.

Recognition comes before legitimacy.  
Legitimacy comes before execution.

Downstream systems may provide execution-boundary control, authority validation, ALLOW / DENY decisions, receipts, proof infrastructure, routing, or enforcement.

The upstream layer and downstream layer are related, but they are not the same.

## Purpose

This document defines the boundary between:

1. upstream recognition, legitimacy, human authority, institutional trust, responsibility, and dignity conditions; and
2. downstream execution-boundary control, proof infrastructure, authority validation, ALLOW / DENY decisions, receipts, and operational enforcement.

This boundary exists to prevent upstream human-recognition frameworks from being unintentionally absorbed into downstream technical systems, vendor materials, client-facing explanations, pilot structures, white-label models, or commercial deployments without attribution, scope, and written agreement.

## Upstream Layer

The upstream layer defines whether authority can validly form before execution.

It includes:

- recognition of the human being;
- recognition of context;
- recognition of responsibility;
- recognition of institutional conditions;
- legitimacy of authority;
- continuity of authority;
- human dignity conditions;
- trust and responsibility conditions;
- the basis on which downstream systems may later act, deny, route, or record.

The upstream layer asks:

- Was the human properly recognized?
- Was the context properly recognized?
- Was responsibility properly recognized?
- Was institutional authority properly formed?
- Is the authority still legitimate at the moment of consequence?
- Should execution inherit this authority, or should it be held, denied, refreshed, or rerouted?

## Downstream Layer

The downstream layer may operationalize, enforce, record, or prove decisions at or near the moment of execution.

It may include:

- execution-boundary control;
- authority validation;
- ALLOW / DENY decisions;
- receipts;
- proof infrastructure;
- routing and blocking;
- evidence that an action was authorized or blocked before consequence.

The downstream layer asks:

- Was the action allowed under the authority condition received?
- Was the authority still valid at execution time?
- Was the action blocked when authority was missing, stale, revoked, mismatched, impaired, or outside scope?
- Can the organization prove what happened afterward?

## Relationship Between the Layers

The upstream layer defines the conditions under which authority may become legitimate.

The downstream layer may enforce or prove whether an action was allowed under a supplied authority condition.

A downstream system can execute correctly and still act on authority that was never properly formed upstream.

For this reason, downstream correctness does not automatically prove upstream legitimacy.

## Non-Absorption Principle

Downstream execution-control or proof systems may reference, interface with, or operationalize upstream recognition and legitimacy conditions.

They do not thereby own, originate, or redefine those upstream conditions.

The upstream framework remains independent unless a separate written agreement defines a specific integration, license, attribution, advisory role, partnership, pilot, or commercial structure.

## General Applicability

This boundary applies generally to downstream execution-control, proof, authority-validation, AI governance, receipt, ALLOW / DENY, and operational enforcement systems.

It is not dependent on any single vendor, tool, infrastructure provider, or implementation.

The purpose is to preserve the distinction between:

- the upstream conditions that make authority legitimate; and
- the downstream systems that may enforce, prove, block, or record execution.
